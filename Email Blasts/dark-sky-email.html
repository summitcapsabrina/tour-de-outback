The Darkest Skies in America Are Right Here in Lake County

Editor: Marie Tucker

Most people have never truly seen the night sky. Light pollution has erased it for roughly 80 percent of North Americans. But in southeastern Oregon, there's a place where the sky still looks the way it did before electricity — before we forgot what was up there.

In March 2024, Lake County earned a designation that put it on the world map: the Oregon Outback International Dark Sky Sanctuary — the largest certified dark sky sanctuary on the planet.

The Milky Way blazes from horizon to horizon, so luminous it casts visible shadows on the desert floor. Astrophotography that takes hours elsewhere takes minutes here.

But the dark sky is only part of the story. Lake County delivers hot springs beneath the stars at Hart Mountain, the towering 2,490-foot wall of Abert Rim, herds of pronghorn sprinting across sagebrush flats, and a silence so deep you can hear your own heartbeat.

Our latest blog post dives deep into what makes this place extraordinary — from the grassroots coalition that fought for the designation, to exactly what you'll see on a clear night, to practical tips for planning your visit.
